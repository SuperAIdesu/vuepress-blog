I was furious on the day I was promoted.

It was a cold and damp Friday morning, and I left home early in the morning to go to my 9 am doctor's appointment. Sat on the waiting room chair, I opened my phone to check work Slack.

"Worth a read team", sent the top executive of the company. Attached was an industry newsletter from an anonymous author. I opened it and browsed the first page, with an AI-generated 2-panel comic prominent.

The first one depicts a printing machine labeled "NYTimes headlines generator", churning out a story about the connection between industry lobbying to the Trump administration and favorable regulation change. In the frame, a Jewish guy raises his hands high in the air, appearing shocked at the paper's distortion of truth.

The second frame shows the same Times printing machine "distorting" the news about "a country defending itself". Below the comics, the front-page article continues to defend both the industry lobbying and Israel's war crimes. I gasped.

"We should sponsor this", said the head of Engineering in the Slack thread.

"We did it a while ago", replied the top executive.

I turned off my phone screen, and attempted to calm my breathing. I told myself I was going to quit soon, and walked into the doctor's room.

It was 3 hours before I met my manager and received the news of promotion.

---

I came to this job after having been driven out of my last one by corporate politics and mounting stress. The new job offer came through a friend, who spoke highly of the recruiting manager. The company was a healthcare AI startup, with a pretty good financial backing despite having only a handful of employees. I happily jumped ship.

However, there were always things that could only be seen from the inside. Like the company's sole supporter and funder was a radical Zionist healthcare-conglomerate-owner-turned billionaire(one hell of a combination, as my therapist commented). Like what I would see once I walked into the office.

The office was at the top of a fancy Hudson Yards tower. The interior was clean, white and sterile. All the startups that the billionaire fund shared the same floor, which was always crowded and buzzing. Here, men were engineers, investors and executives, and women were receptionists, HR and marketing specialists. Almost all were white (likely Jewish), although a few of Asian engineers or analysts were sprinkled into the mix. The head of HR kept at least 3 pairs of heels under her desk, and strode around the office everyday in 4 inch stilettos. On lunchtime, a circle of finance bros and tech bros would convene at the kitchen area, loudly chatting about macroeconomics and latest AI developments. Occasionally one or two woman would sit aside and ask occasional, humble questions.

In the company I was working at, I was the only woman-adjacent employee, let alone queer. Let's not mention the trans or neurodivergent part.

Suddenly I was longing for some performative DEI and loosely-arranged affinity groups.

Soon, I developed techniques to perform my version of professionalism and fend off misogyny. First, minimal conversational niceties: No "I'm doing great, how are you" or "that's a great idea". Second, dress simply and not too feminine. Slacks only, no skirts or dresses. Finally, I used my usually-low-and-sometimes-creaky trans voice to my advantage, and was not afraid to cut people off. After a few weeks, I settled somewhere in between the two modes of workplace gender expression. Non-binary, you may call it.
